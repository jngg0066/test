What is Info Hub?

The Info Hub serves as a comprehensive resource for visitors seeking information on overseas migration trends in Australia, with a particular focus on the state of Victoria. It provides a detailed analysis of migration data, showcasing the net overseas migration figures over the past decade and offering insights into the significant increase in migrant arrivals, especially post-COVID-19.

The hub also delves into the demographics of Australia's overseas-born population, highlighting the top 5 developed countries of origin and their respective growth patterns over the years.

Overseas Migration Trend in Australia (2013-2023)

Insights into Australia's Migration Trends (2022-23)

Record Net Migration: Australia experienced its highest ever net gain of 518,000 people.

Surge in Arrivals: Migrant arrivals increased by 73% to 737,000, with most being temporary visa holders (554,000).

COVID-19 Impact: Pandemic restrictions caused a decline in migration in 2020-21, but numbers are rebounding.

Key Contributors: International students, working holiday makers, and skilled workers were major contributors to the rise in arrivals.

Permanent Visa and Citizen Arrivals: Arrivals of permanent visa holders and Australian citizens remained below pre-pandemic levels.

Decrease in Departures: Migrant departures decreased by 2% to 219,000, contributing to the high net migration.

Future Trends: Anticipated departures of temporary visa holders, such as international students, may reduce net migration in the future.

Overseas-born Population: Top 5 Developed Countries in Victoria, Australia

Insights into Australia's Migration Trends (2022-23)

Proportion of Overseas-born Population:

  • In 2021, 29.3% of Australia's population was born overseas, with variations across states and territories. At the national level in 2021, the largest groups of overseas-born individuals were from England, India, China, and New Zealand.

    • Country of Birth Trends:

  • India - The population born in India has shown substantial growth, increasing from 31.14 thousand in 2001 to 272.25 thousand in 2021.
  • China - Similarly, the population born in China has seen steady growth, rising from 38.86 thousand in 2001 to 182.14 thousand in 2021.
  • Vietnam - The population born in Vietnam has exhibited a fluctuating trend, starting at 59.49 thousand in 2001, dropping slightly to 76.23 thousand in 2011, and then increasing to 99.35 thousand in 2021.
  • Sri Lanka - The population born in Sri Lanka has shown consistent growth, rising from 29.86 thousand in 2001 to 72.1 thousand in 2021.
  • Philippines - Similarly, the population born in the Philippines has shown steady growth, increasing from 24.46 thousand in 2001 to 71.25 thousand in 2021.

    • These trends indicate a general increase in the population born in these countries over the past two decades, reflecting Australia's diverse and multicultural society.

    Challenges

    Children of new Australian migrants often encounter language barriers that affect their social and academic interactions. Strategies such as utilizing translation apps, watching local media with subtitles, and actively noting new vocabulary can help them overcome these challenges. Moreover, while initially intimidating, grasping Australian slang becomes more manageable through exposure and inquiry, ultimately supporting smoother integration into daily life.

    Example: Though India boasts the second-largest English-speaking population, fluency remains low (10%). Educational and cultural barriers, along with historical baggage, make teaching English a complex task.

    Culture shock refers to the emotional response triggered by encountering unfamiliar customs and behaviours in a new country. Australian culture, characterized by its casual and individualistic nature, may contrast with the collectivistic norms prevalent in some Asian cultures. Children may need to adapt to less formal greetings, reduced emphasis on hierarchy (e.g., respect for elders, teachers), and a more direct communication style. Acquiring an understanding of social norms, such as direct communication and humour, aids in adaptation. Additionally, prioritizing social connections and engaging in activities like sports clubs facilitates integration into Australian culture.

    Example: A Scanlon Foundation study finds mainland Chinese immigrants in Australia struggle more to integrate than some East Asians. Language barriers, social isolation, especially for older migrants, and rising discrimination threaten their progress.
